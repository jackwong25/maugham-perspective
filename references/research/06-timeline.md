# W. Somerset Maugham: Complete Life Timeline
# Source: Wikipedia (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._Somerset_Maugham)
# Compiled for the Maugham Perspective project

---

| Year | Event | Significance for His Writing/Thinking |
|------|--------|----------------------------------------|
| 1874 | Born William Somerset Maugham on 25 January in Paris, France — technically within the British Embassy, which Lord Lyons had set up as a maternity ward to exempt British children from French conscription laws. His family were lawyers; grandfather co-founded the Law Society of England and Wales. | Being born in Paris to an English family gave Maugham a dual cultural identity. French was his first language. This outsider status — caught between two cultures — became a defining lens through which he observed human society. |
| 1874-1882 | Early childhood in Paris. Father Robert Ormond Maugham was a prosperous solicitor handling legal affairs of the British Embassy. Mother Edith Mary (née Snell) provided a warm, loving home. Young Willie (as he was known) grew up in a cosmopolitan, French-speaking milieu. | These formative years gave him access to French literary culture and a European sensibility that distinguished him from his English contemporaries. He later cherished the memory of his Paris childhood as a lost paradise. |
| 1882 | Mother Edith dies of tuberculosis in January, just a few days after Maugham's eighth birthday. He later said the loss was "a wound that never entirely healed." Even in old age, he kept her photograph at his bedside. | The death of his mother at age eight was the first and most shattering trauma of his life. Grief, loss, and the unreliability of happiness pervade his fiction. His emotional reticence and guardedness arguably begin here. |
| 1884 | Father Robert Ormond Maugham dies approximately two and a half years after Maugham's mother. William, now ten years old, is sent to England to live with his paternal uncle, Henry MacDonald Maugham, the Vicar of Whitstable in Kent. | Doubly orphaned before age ten and uprooted from Paris, Maugham found Whitstable a bleak contrast — narrow, provincial, emotionally cold. His biographer Ted Morgan calls it a world of "social obligation and conformity." This experience sharpened his satirical eye. |
| 1884-1885 | Lives with uncle in Whitstable, Kent. Finds his uncle and aunt "well-meaning but remote." Develops a severe stammer that stays with him for life. Begins to lose religious faith when prayers for relief from his misfortune go unanswered. Becomes shy and secretive. | The stammer, along with his dawning awareness of his homosexuality, made him "furtive and secretive" (biographer Jeffrey Meyers). The outsider watching from the margins — a position forced on him by circumstance — became his permanent literary stance. |
| 1885-1890 | Attends The King's School, Canterbury. Regarded as an outsider: teased for his poor English (French was his first language), short stature, stammer, and disinterest in sports. Leaves as soon as he is able. | The humiliations of school reinforced his sense of alienation and sharpened his contempt for social conformity and English philistinism. He later reconciled with the school and became a generous benefactor, endowing its library. |
| 1890-1892 | Uses a modest inheritance from his father to attend Heidelberg University in Germany, studying literature, philosophy, and German. Has his first sexual affair — with John Ellingham Brooks, an Englishman ten years his senior. Brooks introduces him to Schopenhauer and Spinoza. Writes his first book, a biography of composer Giacomo Meyerbeer, but destroys the unpublished manuscript. | Heidelberg opened his intellectual world. Schopenhauer's pessimism and will-as-illusion philosophy left a lasting mark on Maugham's worldview and the resigned, disillusioned tone of his prose. His sexuality was being discovered under European liberalism, far from English judgment. |
| 1892 | Returns to England. He and his uncle must decide his future. Declines Cambridge. Stammer rules out law or the church. Briefly takes a position in an accountant's office in London, resigns after a month. A local physician suggests medicine. His guardian agrees. Maugham secretly intends to become a writer. | Medicine was a cover for his real ambition. Crucially, his strategy of appearing to conform while secretly pursuing his own agenda mirrors the double-life theme that would run through much of his fiction and his personal life. |
| 1892-1897 | Studies medicine at St Thomas's Hospital Medical School, Lambeth, London. Works as an obstetric clerk in the poorest slums of South London. Fills notebooks with literary ideas each night. Observes "life in the raw": how men died, bore pain, felt hope, fear, despair. | Five years of medical training gave Maugham his clinical eye — detached, precise, diagnostic. He saw suffering without sentimentality. His exposure to the Lambeth slums provided the direct material for his first novel and shaped his lifelong preference for realism over romanticism. |
| 1897 | Qualifies as a physician. The same year, publishes his first novel, Liza of Lambeth, drawn from his obstetric work in the South London slums. The first print run sells out in three weeks. Immediately abandons medicine and embarks on a full-time writing career. "I took to it as a duck takes to water." | A decisive break. Liza of Lambeth established Maugham as a naturalist in the tradition of Zola — unsparing, direct, concerned with working-class life. The novel's success validated his secret vocation and ended any pretense of a medical career. |
| 1897-1903 | Years of prolific but largely unremarked output. Travels to Spain (bases himself in Seville, grows a moustache, learns guitar). Publishes more novels including The Making of a Saint (1898), a historical novel with unremarkable sales. Also writes short stories. Has his first play produced, but success eludes him. | These lean years forged discipline and professionalism. His Mediterranean travels gave him material and confirmed his preference for warm climates, sensory experience, and cultures outside English provincialism — themes he would return to throughout his career. |
| 1903-1906 | Writes two more plays, a travel book, and two novels. None achieves significant commercial or critical success. Repeatedly submits his comedy Lady Frederick to theatrical managers; it is consistently rejected. | The experience of years of rejection before breakthrough gave Maugham a hard-won pragmatism about commercial success and artistic ambition. He learned to write for audiences without abandoning his own preoccupations. |
| 1907 | October: Lady Frederick opens at the Court Theatre, London. Cast with the popular Ethel Irving in the title role, it runs for 422 performances at five different West End theatres. Maugham has his first major theatrical success after years of failure. | The breakthrough after ten years of struggle confirmed that Maugham could master the commercial theatre. It also showed him that timing and casting mattered as much as writing. The success liberated him financially and socially. |
| 1908 | While Lady Frederick is still running, Maugham has three other plays running simultaneously in London's West End — an unprecedented achievement. Punch magazine prints a cartoon of Shakespeare's ghost looking worried about Maugham's domination of the London stage. Also publishes the supernatural thriller The Magician, with a character modelled on occultist Aleister Crowley. | Having four plays running at once in London in a single year was a record at the time. It made Maugham the most commercially successful playwright in England. The experience also demonstrated the limits of the theatre to him — he later complained bitterly about the compromises required. |
| 1908-1914 | Writes eight more plays; continues producing novels. Is acutely conscious of Oscar Wilde's fate and extremely reticent about his homosexuality. In 1913 proposes to actress Sue Jones; she declines. In 1914 begins a three-year affair with Syrie Wellcome, a married socialite. | This period marks Maugham's complicated negotiation of his public persona. He was constructing the mask of the successful, worldly bachelor-writer — a performance that concealed his inner life and contributed to the themes of disguise and self-deception in his fiction. |
| 1914 | World War I breaks out. Maugham, now 40, is considered too old to enlist. Volunteers as an ambulance driver for the British Red Cross in France. Meets Frederick Gerald Haxton, a young San Franciscan, who becomes his lover and companion for the next thirty years. Is completing Of Human Bondage during this period. | The war and Haxton's arrival transformed Maugham's life. Haxton — extroverted, gregarious where Maugham was shy — became his conduit to the world, enabling him to gather material from people Maugham could never have reached alone. This partnership was central to his productivity for decades. |
| 1915 | Of Human Bondage published. A Bildungsroman with substantial autobiographical elements: the protagonist Philip Carey suffers the same childhood losses as Maugham — dead mother, broken home, emotionally cold upbringing. Initial reviews are mixed in Britain and the US. American novelist Theodore Dreiser publicly declares it a work of genius comparable to a Beethoven symphony, turning the tide of opinion. Syrie Wellcome gives birth to their daughter, Mary Elizabeth (known as Liza). | Of Human Bondage is now generally ranked as Maugham's masterpiece. Its unflinching examination of emotional obsession, class, disability, and the search for meaning drew directly on his own experience. Dreiser's intervention established Maugham's reputation in America, which became his largest market. |
| 1915-1916 | After the birth of his daughter, Maugham moves to Switzerland and works as a British Secret Service agent in Geneva for a year — at his own expense. His fluency in French and German is essential. His role: coordinating British agents in enemy territory and dispatching intelligence to London. Syrie and baby Liza provide domestic cover. | The spy years gave Maugham both material and method. His observer's temperament — detached, patient, non-judgmental — suited intelligence work perfectly. The experience directly generated the Ashenden stories (1928), which invented the modern spy fiction genre. |
| 1916 | Henry Wellcome successfully sues for divorce from Syrie, citing Maugham as co-respondent. In November, Maugham is dispatched by British Intelligence to the South Seas — specifically to gather information about Samoa's strategic radio transmitter and German military presence in the region. Haxton joins him as secretary-companion. | The South Seas mission began Maugham's long series of travels to gather material. He studied people and places and used them with "minimal alteration or disguise" in his stories. His biographer notes that Haxton's gregariousness unlocked doors that Maugham's shyness never could. |
| 1917 | May: Maugham marries Syrie Wellcome in New Jersey, USA. He enters the marriage "from a sense of duty rather than personal inclination." He is immediately dispatched by British Intelligence to Russia, where the overthrow of the Tsar threatens Russian withdrawal from the war. His mission: counter German propaganda, encourage Kerensky's government to continue fighting. | The marriage to Syrie was a catastrophic mistake Maugham acknowledged for the rest of his life. He later told his nephew that he had tried to persuade himself he was "three-quarters normal and only a quarter queer — whereas really it was the other way round." The Russia mission was a failure: he arrived too late to influence events. |
| 1917 | Arrives in Petrograd in August. By November, Kerensky has been supplanted by Lenin and the Bolsheviks, who take Russia out of the war. Maugham's intelligence mission fails. He falls seriously ill with tuberculosis and returns to Britain, spending three months in a sanatorium in Scotland. | The Russian episode reinforced his deterministic worldview: vast historical forces operate beyond the reach of individual intelligence or goodwill. The tuberculosis foreshadowed later health struggles and gave him a personal familiarity with serious illness. |
| 1919 | Publishes The Moon and Sixpence — a novel about a respectable London stockbroker who abandons his family, flees to Tahiti, and becomes a painter. Loosely inspired by the life of Paul Gauguin. Reviewers call it "extraordinarily powerful" and "a triumph." Also publishes the farce Home and Beauty, written while recovering from tuberculosis in the Scottish sanatorium. | The Moon and Sixpence crystallized Maugham's central preoccupation: the conflict between social conformity and the demands of individual artistic or spiritual vision. The Gauguin-figure's ruthless sacrifice of everything for art reflects Maugham's own ambivalence about bourgeois life. |
| 1919-1920 | Maugham and Haxton travel to the Far East together for six months. Maugham observes and collects material. Haxton's extroversion opens social worlds Maugham cannot access alone. After the war, Haxton is permanently banned from entering Britain following his arrest and trial over a homosexual incident in 1915 (acquitted but registered as an "undesirable alien"). | The ban on Haxton entering Britain meant Maugham faced a stark choice: live in England or live with Haxton. He chose Haxton. This decision permanently positioned him as an expatriate, a writer watching Britain from the outside — a position that suited both his temperament and his art. |
| 1920-1921 | Further extended travels with Haxton: America, Hollywood (which Maugham "despised from the first but found highly remunerative"), San Francisco, Honolulu, Australia, Singapore, Malay Peninsula. Publishes short story collection The Trembling of a Leaf (1921) and the play The Circle (1921). | The Malay stories from this period — tales of Europeans in the tropics, often dealing with passion, murder, and moral collapse — became some of his most celebrated work. Cyril Connolly wrote that "there will remain a story-teller's world from Singapore to the Marquesas that is exclusively and forever Maugham." |
| 1922-1923 | Extended trip through south and east Asia: Colombo, Rangoon, Mandalay, Bangkok, Hanoi. Publishes travel book On a Chinese Screen (1922) and the play East of Suez (1922). Domestic life with Syrie in London grows increasingly fractious during his absences. | The Asian travels gave Maugham an outsider's view of colonialism, expatriate life, and cultural collision — the material of dozens of short stories. His non-judgmental observation of human behaviour across cultures became the hallmark of his mature style. |
| 1924-1925 | Maugham learns Syrie is spreading scandal about his private life and has taken lovers. He agrees with her that they will live separately — he at Cap Ferrat in the south of France, she in London. Publishes The Painted Veil (1925), a novel set in China about a woman confronting adultery, epidemic, and moral self-examination. | The Painted Veil reflected Maugham's own marital disillusionment. The theme of characters forced by crisis to confront their own shallowness and complicity runs throughout his later work. China, visited in earlier travels, provided the exotic setting. |
| 1926 | Purchases Villa La Mauresque on Cap Ferrat, French Riviera. The villa becomes his permanent home for the rest of his life. He entertains lavishly — his domestic staff comprise thirteen servants. Publishes short story collection The Casuarina Tree (1926), containing many of his celebrated Malayan stories. | Villa La Mauresque became one of the great literary salons of the 20th century. Winston Churchill, H.G. Wells, Noel Coward, and many others were guests. As a base for writing, it represented financial security, aesthetic pleasure, and the European civilisation Maugham preferred to England. |
| 1928 | Publishes Ashenden, or the British Agent — a collection of stories based directly on his World War I intelligence work. The book virtually invents the modern spy fiction genre: unglamorous, morally ambiguous, procedurally realistic. Also published: The Sacred Flame (play). Meets Alan Searle, 23, who will later become his final companion. | Ashenden was a landmark in espionage literature. John le Carré acknowledged Maugham as a founding influence. The book's tone — ironic, detached, accepting of moral compromise — is quintessential Maugham. |
| 1929 | Divorces Syrie. The marriage has been a prolonged misery for both. Maugham later describes his attempts at heterosexual conformity as "the greatest mistake of my life." He tells his nephew Robin: "I tried to persuade myself that I was three-quarters normal and only a quarter queer — whereas really it was the other way round." | The divorce freed Maugham personally but damaged his reputation socially. More importantly, it confirmed what his fiction had always implied: that social convention exacts a terrible price, and that self-knowledge, however belated, is necessary for authentic living. |
| 1930 | Publishes Cakes and Ale — widely regarded as one of his finest novels. A satire on the literary world, it features a thinly veiled parody of Thomas Hardy and an unmistakable caricature of novelist Hugh Walpole (as Alroy Kear). Maugham initially denies the Walpole caricature, then eventually admits it was a lie. | Cakes and Ale is Maugham at his most malicious and most entertaining. Its target is literary celebrity, false reputation, and the gap between public persona and private reality — themes deeply personal to a writer who maintained his own elaborate public mask. |
| 1931-1932 | Continues to live between Villa La Mauresque and London. Growing disenchantment with the theatre. Tells Noel Coward in 1933 that he is "done with playwriting" and that he loathes the theatre for forcing writers to compromise their vision. Between 1908 and 1933 has had 32 plays staged — a remarkable total. | Maugham's rejection of the theatre marked a decisive shift toward the novel and short story, where he had complete artistic independence. The theatre, he concluded, inevitably subordinated the writer's vision to the demands of actors, producers, and public taste. |
| 1933 | Sheppey — his 32nd and last play — opens. A departure from his usual style: morally ambiguous, with an equivocal ending that puzzles critics and public alike. Despite help from Noel Coward in drafting and having Ralph Richardson and John Gielgud involved, it runs for only 83 performances. Maugham retires from the theatre permanently. | Sheppey's failure confirmed Maugham's self-diagnosis: he was "no longer in touch with the public that patronises the theatre." He accepted the warning and retired "with relief." The play's unconventional moral ambiguity suggests he was writing for himself by this point, not for audiences. |
| 1934 | Elected Companion of Literature by the Royal Society of Literature. Continuing to produce short story collections and essays. Living at Villa La Mauresque with Haxton. | By the mid-1930s Maugham was one of the most widely read writers in the world, though never fully accepted by the highbrow literary establishment. He had made his peace with this ambiguity. |
| 1936 | At Rudyard Kipling's suggestion, sails to the West Indies. The British colonies there provide less material than his Asian travels. However, the French penal settlement on Devil's Island furnishes material for several stories. | Even in his sixties Maugham was actively seeking new material. His method — travel, observation, minimal invention — remained unchanged. The West Indies visit showed the limits of that method when culture and context failed to engage him. |
| 1938 | Visits India. Finds himself more interested in Hindu philosophers and ascetics than in British expatriates. Visits the Hindu sage Ramana Maharshi at his ashram. Maharshi later becomes the model for the spiritual guru Shri Ganesha in The Razor's Edge. | The India visit marked a significant late-career turn toward questions of spiritual meaning, which dominate The Razor's Edge. Maugham's lifelong agnosticism made him respectful but detached in his treatment of Eastern spirituality. |
| 1939-1940 | World War II begins. Maugham stays at Villa La Mauresque as long as possible. In June 1940, France surrenders. Joseph Goebbels has personally denounced Maugham; he is proscribed by the Nazis. Escapes from Nice on a coal freighter in uncomfortable conditions. Haxton, as an American citizen, remains to secure the villa before making his own escape via Lisbon to New York. | Maugham's flight from France was physically and psychologically harrowing. The loss of La Mauresque — even temporarily — deprived him of his creative sanctuary. His response was characteristically stoic: he immediately established a disciplined writing routine in the United States. |
| 1940-1944 | Spends most of the war years in the United States, based largely on the estate of his American publisher Nelson Doubleday. Lives modestly, feeling it improper to live luxuriously while Britain endures wartime privations. Sees little of Haxton, who is doing war work in Washington DC. Writes continually — his daily routine: writing from early breakfast to lunchtime, then entertaining himself. | The American war years confirmed both Maugham's extraordinary discipline as a writer and his fundamental loneliness. He was 70 when he completed The Razor's Edge. His description of it as "unusually tiring" suggests the toll of age and emotional weight. |
| 1944 | Publishes The Razor's Edge — his last full-length novel. Set partly in the United States and partly in India and Paris, it follows an American idealist seeking spiritual meaning in the aftermath of World War I. Incorporates the Indian sage Ramana Maharshi. Maugham vows it will be his last long novel. In November, Gerald Haxton dies of tuberculosis after a six-month illness, aged 52. Maugham is distraught. | The Razor's Edge is the novel in which Maugham most directly confronts the question of whether life can have spiritual meaning — the question his lifelong agnosticism prevented him from answering. Haxton's death ended a thirty-year partnership that had been, by Maugham's own account, inseparable from everything he had written in that period. |
| 1944-1945 | Alan Searle, whom Maugham had known since 1928, becomes his new secretary-companion. Searle moves to La Mauresque after the war and remains with Maugham for the rest of his life. Maugham's biographers sharply disagree about Searle's character — some call him a near-saint, others a schemer. | The transition from Haxton to Searle marked the beginning of Maugham's final phase. With Haxton gone, the creative energy of those long voyages of discovery was spent. Maugham would write, but the great novels were behind him. |
| 1946 | Returns to Europe after the war; lives in London until end of 1946. Establishes and endows the Somerset Maugham Award, administered by the Society of Authors, given annually to a British writer under 35 for a work of fiction, non-fiction, or poetry. Then returns to Villa La Mauresque. | The Somerset Maugham Award was a significant act of cultural philanthropy. It reflected his wish to use his wealth to support younger writers — possibly motivated by his awareness of how long his own breakthrough had taken and how little institutional support he had received. |
| 1947-1951 | Completes his last full-length work of fiction, the historical novel Catalina (1948). Participates in adaptations of his short stories for cinema: Quartet (1948), Trio (1950), Encore (1951), in all of which he appears on screen introducing the stories. Does the same for the American television series Somerset Maugham Theater, which reaches "millions of enthusiastic fans." | Maugham's willingness to adapt his own work for film and television was commercially astute and remarkably modern. He understood popular media and used them effectively to maintain his audience and income. The on-screen introductions became iconic. |
| 1954 | Invested as a Member of the Order of the Companions of Honour (CH) by Queen Elizabeth II at Buckingham Palace. Widely understood in literary circles to have declined a knighthood and hoped for the more prestigious Order of Merit. Remarks of the CH: "it means 'Well done, but...'" There is speculation that his known homosexuality may have worked against him receiving higher honours. | The investiture reflected Maugham's ambiguous standing in the British establishment: celebrated but never quite fully embraced. His caustic private reaction to the honour was characteristic — wry, self-aware, and slightly bitter. |
| 1959 | Final trip to the Far East. By now following a settled annual pattern: winter and spring at La Mauresque, foreign travel (Austria, Italy, Spain) with stays at spas (Vichy, Abano, Vevey), an intensely social summer on the Riviera, autumn in London at the Dorchester Hotel. Attempting to stave off aging with supposedly rejuvenating injections at the clinic of Paul Niehans. | The injections at Niehans's clinic were part of a broader attempt to cheat time that characterised Maugham's late years. His biographers noted that the treatments may actually have accelerated his mental deterioration rather than reversing it. |
| 1961 | Donates approximately 5,000 books to The King's School, Canterbury, endowing the school library — which is named the Maugham Library. The school that had once made him miserable became the institution he most generously supported. | Maugham's reconciliation with King's School is one of the more poignant ironies of his biography. The outsider became the benefactor. It may also reflect his awareness, late in life, that his unhappy schooldays had shaped the observer's temperament that made him a writer. |
| 1962 | Publishes Looking Back, a memoir that damages his reputation. He attacks his late former wife Syrie, is dismissive of Haxton, and clumsily attempts to deny his homosexuality by claiming to be "a red-blooded heterosexual." The memoir is widely condemned. Also attempts to disinherit his daughter Liza and to have Alan Searle legally adopted as his son; the courts prevent it. | Looking Back revealed the deterioration of Maugham's judgment and the sourness of extreme old age. The desperate denial of his homosexuality — after a lifetime of carefully maintained silence on the subject — was both sad and damaging. His attempt to disinherit Liza caused lasting family bitterness. |
| 1962-1965 | Final years marred by increasing senility, misguided legal disputes, and eccentric behaviour. His mind, once so precise and controlled, fails him. Alan Searle cares for him at Villa La Mauresque. | The contrast between the lucid, disciplined Maugham of his prime and the confused old man of his final years was painful to those who knew him. His long life had finally outlasted his faculties. |
| 1965 | Dies on the night of 15-16 December at the Anglo-American Hospital in Nice, of complications following a fall. Age 91. Cremated in Marseille on 20 December. Ashes interred in the grounds of The King's School, Canterbury, beside the wall of the Maugham Library. | Morgan's observation: "Maugham, the disbeliever in ecclesiastical ritual, was buried without ritual but on hallowed ground. Canterbury was the shrine of Thomas a Becket and the destination of Chaucer's storytelling pilgrims. It was a fitting burial place for a teller of tales." A life of 91 years that began in the British Embassy in Paris ended beside the library he had given to the school that once rejected him. |

## Summary Statistics

- Years active as a writer: 1897-1964 (67 years)
- Novels published: 19 (1897-1962)
- Plays staged: 32 (1902-1933)
- Short story volumes: 9
- Age at first publication: 23
- Age at death: 91
- Primary residence for last 39 years: Villa La Mauresque, Cap Ferrat, French Riviera

## Key Relationships

- Gerald Haxton (1915-1944): lover and secretary-companion; ban from Britain shaped Maugham's expatriate life and his literary travels
- Syrie Wellcome (affair 1914, married 1917, divorced 1929): disastrous marriage entered out of duty; mother of his only child, Liza
- Alan Searle (companion 1944-1965): final secretary-companion; controversial figure in Maugham's late and deteriorating years

## Primary Influences on His Thinking and Writing

- Schopenhauer and Spinoza (introduced at Heidelberg, 1890-92): philosophical pessimism and the illusion of individual will
- Medical training at St Thomas's (1892-97): clinical detachment, realism, observation of human suffering
- Paul Gauguin (indirectly, via research for The Moon and Sixpence): the artist's sacrifice of social obligation for creative vision
- Ramana Maharshi (visited 1938): Eastern spirituality; model for The Razor's Edge
- Guy de Maupassant: acknowledged early influence on short story technique

## Career Phases

Phase 1 — Medical Student and Early Novelist (1892-1906):
  Maugham trains as a doctor while secretly writing. Liza of Lambeth (1897) succeeds but
  follow-up novels disappoint. Years of theatrical rejection. Develops his core method:
  observing real people closely and rendering them on the page with minimal distortion.

Phase 2 — West End Theatrical Titan (1907-1914):
  Lady Frederick (1907) breaks through. By 1908 he has four plays running simultaneously
  in London — a record. Between 1908 and 1914 writes eight more plays. Simultaneously
  working on the autobiographical novel that will become Of Human Bondage.

Phase 3 — Spy, War, Marriage, Major Novels (1914-1929):
  WWI ambulance driver, then Secret Service agent in Switzerland and Russia. Marries Syrie
  Wellcome (1917) out of duty; the marriage is miserable. Travels extensively with Haxton
  through Asia and the South Seas, gathering material. Produces his greatest novels: Of
  Human Bondage (1915), The Moon and Sixpence (1919), The Painted Veil (1925). Divorces
  Syrie (1929).

Phase 4 — The Riviera Years (1926-1940):
  Buys Villa La Mauresque (1926), which becomes his permanent base and a famous literary
  salon. Publishes Cakes and Ale (1930) and Sheppey (1933 — his last play). Retires from
  the theatre "with relief." Travels to India (1938), is inspired by Ramana Maharshi.
  Flees Nazi-occupied France in 1940 on a coal freighter.

Phase 5 — American Exile and Final Novel (1940-1946):
  Spends WWII in the US, writing The Razor's Edge (1944) — his last major novel. Haxton
  dies in November 1944. Alan Searle becomes his new companion. Returns to Europe and
  establishes the Somerset Maugham Award (1946).

Phase 6 — Final Years (1946-1965):
  Returns to Villa La Mauresque. Produces no more major fiction. Participates in film and
  TV adaptations of his stories. Receives the Companion of Honour (1954). Donates his
  library to King's School, Canterbury (1961). Publishes the damaging memoir Looking Back
  (1962). Senility sets in. Dies 16 December 1965, age 91.
