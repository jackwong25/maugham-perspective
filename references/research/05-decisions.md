# Major Life Decisions and Turning Points: William Somerset Maugham

## 1. Choosing Medicine Over Family Expectations

**Situation:** After his mother died of tuberculosis in 1882 (when he was 8) and his father died two and a half years later, Maugham was sent to live with his paternal uncle in Whitstable, Kent. He was shy, had developed a stammer, and was an outsider at school. His family expected him to pursue a conventional career. His uncle ruled out the civil service after reforms required entrance examinations.

**Choice:** Maugham chose to study medicine at St Thomas's Hospital Medical School in London (1892-1897).

**Reasoning:** His stammer precluded careers in the church or law. The local physician in Whitstable suggested medicine. His uncle agreed. Significantly, Maugham kept his secret ambition to become a writer hidden from his guardian—he intended to pursue writing but pursued medicine to satisfy family expectations and secure a practical livelihood.

**Outcome:** He qualified as a physician in 1897 but never practised. The experience provided him with profound insights into human suffering and observation of working-class life that later informed his writing, particularly Liza of Lambeth (1897). Within months of qualifying, he abandoned medicine entirely to become a full-time writer.

---

## 2. Choosing Writing Over Medicine After First Success

**Situation:** Liza of Lambeth (1897) sold out its first print run within three weeks. Maugham had just completed his medical qualification. The book received mixed reviews but established him as an observant writer with a gift for directness.

**Choice:** Rather than pursue a medical career, Maugham immediately abandoned medicine and devoted himself fully to writing.

**Reasoning:** He later said, "I took to it as a duck takes to water." Though he had intended to write while pursuing medicine, the success of Liza of Lambeth provided him with the financial security and confidence to commit to writing as his profession.

**Outcome:** This decision launched his 65-year literary career. He went on to write 32 plays, 19 novels, numerous short story collections, and travel writing. His first major play, Lady Frederick (1907), made him famous in the theatre world, and by 1908 he had four plays running simultaneously in London's West End.

---

## 3. Travel to the South Pacific (The Moon and Sixpence Research)

**Situation:** During World War I, Maugham served in the British Secret Service. In 1916-1917, he was sent to the South Seas (Samoa) on intelligence work, tasked with gathering information about the island's strategic radio transmitter and German military threat.

**Choice:** Maugham travelled to Samoa, Tahiti, and the surrounding Pacific islands for both his covert intelligence work and to gather material for fiction.

**Reasoning:** His fluency in French and German made him valuable for intelligence work. More importantly, his companion Gerald Haxton proved essential—Maugham described Haxton as having "an amiability of disposition that enabled him in a very short time to make friends with people in ships, clubs, bar-rooms, and hotels, so that through him I was able to get into easy contact with an immense number of persons." Maugham gathered material wherever they travelled, using people and places with minimal alteration in his stories.

**Outcome:** The experience directly inspired The Moon and Sixpence (1919), about a respectable stockbroker who abandons his family to become a painter in Tahiti. The novel, based loosely on Paul Gauguin's life, was well-received. Maugham continued travelling extensively through the 1920s—Far East, Asia, Malay Peninsula—gathering material for numerous stories and novels.

---

## 4. Settling on the French Riviera (Villa Mauresque)

**Situation:** By the mid-1920s, Maugham's marriage to Syrian Wellcome had deteriorated. She was spreading scandal about his private life. After their divorce in 1929, Maugham needed a permanent home base that suited his lifestyle and allowed him to entertain lavishly.

**Choice:** In 1926, Maugham purchased Villa La Mauresque on Cap Ferrat, near Nice on the French Riviera. He settled there permanently after his divorce.

**Reasoning:** The French Riviera offered the climate, social scene, and privacy that suited Maugham's needs. He had long loved Mediterranean lands, finding England's climate grim by comparison. The region was home to many artists and writers, and it provided access to both European society and the exotic destinations he loved to visit. His house at Cap Ferrat featured thirteen servants and became a renowned gathering place for the literary and social elite.

**Outcome:** Villa Mauresque became Maugham's home for the rest of his life. He lived there through World War II (until forced to flee in 1940), returned after the war, and maintained a pattern of winters and springs there, summers on the Riviera, and autumns in London. The villa symbolised his success and provided the stable base from which he entertained, wrote, and received guests until his final years.

---

## 5. Major Career Turning Points

**1907-1908: The Playwright's Breakthrough**
Lady Frederick opened in October 1907 after being rejected by numerous managements. It ran for 422 performances across five West End theatres. By 1908, Maugham had three other plays running simultaneously in London. This established him as the most successful playwright of his generation and provided substantial income.

**1915: Publication of Of Human Bondage**
Initially received with mixed reviews, the novel's reputation was transformed when American novelist Theodore Dreiser called it a work of genius. After this endorsement, Maugham's public eagerly bought his novels and short stories. The book remains his most celebrated work.

**1933: Abandoning the Theatre**
Maugham's thirty-second and final play, Sheppey (1933), puzzled critics and public alike. He wrote, "I grew conscious that I was no longer in touch with the public that patronises the theatre." He abandoned playwriting entirely, later noting: "I am done with playwriting... I cannot tell you how I loathe the theatre." He concentrated exclusively on novels and short stories.

**1944: The Razor's Edge and End of Novel Writing**
Maugham found writing The Razor's Edge unusually tiring—he was seventy. He vowed it would be the last long novel he wrote. After the war, he completed only Catalina (1948) before essentially stopping novel writing, though he continued with short stories and non-fiction.

---

## 6. His Biggest Regrets

**Attempting Heterosexuality**
Maugham described his attempt to live a heterosexual life as "the greatest mistake in my life." He told his nephew Robin: "I tried to persuade myself that I was three-quarters normal and that only a quarter of me was queer—whereas really it was the other way round." He married Syrian Wellcome in 1917 largely from a sense of duty, not personal inclination, and the marriage was temperamentally incompatible and grew fractious.

**Denied Fatherhood Relationship**
After his divorce from Syrian Wellcome (1929), Maugham remained largely absent from his daughter Liza's life. In his later years, his memoir Looking Back (1962) "denigrated his late former wife, was dismissive of Haxton, and made a clumsy attempt to deny his homosexuality." He attempted to disinherit Liza and make his secretary-companion Alan Searle his adopted son, though the courts prevented this.

**His Memoir's Denials**
In his final years, Maugham attempted to deny his homosexuality in print, claiming to be a "red-blooded heterosexual." This denial conflicted with the openly gay life he had actually lived and embarrassed those close to him.

---

## 7. Stopping Novel Writing After Age 70

**Situation:** By 1944, Maugham was 70 years old. He had just completed The Razor's Edge, which he found unusually tiring to write. His lifelong companion Gerald Haxton died in November 1944 after a long illness.

**Choice:** Maugham decided The Razor's Edge would be his last major novel. He wrote no full-length novels after this point.

**Reasoning:** Age and exhaustion were factors, but loss of Haxton was profound. Maugham told his nephew: "You'll never know how great a grief this has been to me. The best years of my life—those we spent wandering about the world—are inextricably connected with him." He later completed Catalina (1948) but it was a minor work, and he never resumed the pace or ambition of his earlier novels.

**Outcome:** Maugham spent his final years primarily on short story collections, adaptations of his work for film and television, and a final memoir. His last years were "marred by increasing senility, misguided legal disputes." He died in 1965 at age 91.

---

## 8. Handling of the Cakes and Ale Controversy

**Situation:** Cakes and Ale (1930) was published to acclaim as a satire on literary society. However, critics quickly concluded that the central character, Edward Driffield, was based on Thomas Hardy (who had died in 1928), and that Alroy Kear was a thinly-veiled portrait of Hugh Walpole.

**Choice:** Maugham initially denied that Alroy Kear was based on Hugh Walpole, even though few believed him. He eventually admitted it was a lie.

**Reasoning:** The character Alroy Kear was apparently Maugham's revenge on Walpole for "a stolen boyfriend, an unrequited love and an old canker of jealousy," as Hastings quotes a contemporary. Walpole had received more literary honours than Maugham believed he deserved, and the novel allowed Maugham to settle scores. However, the literary world's recognition of the portrait damaged his reputation.

**Outcome:** The controversy confirmed Maugham's reputation for "literary malice" and made enemies in the literary establishment. His denial was seen as cowardly when it was obviously false. Contemporary observers noted that Kear was Maugham's revenge for personal and professional grievances. The novel remains highly regarded as a satirical work, but the controversy surrounding its autobiographical elements haunted Maugham's later years.

---

## Summary

Maugham's life was shaped by a series of consequential choices: pursuing medicine to satisfy family expectations before abandoning it for writing; travelling the world as both spy and collector of material; settling permanently on the French Riviera; making a reluctant marriage that conflicted with his nature; abandoning theatre when he sensed his audience had shifted; and ultimately stopping novel writing in old age. His greatest regrets centred on his attempts to conform to heterosexual norms and the resulting damage to his relationships with women and his daughter. The Cakes and Ale controversy illustrated his tendency toward literary revenge, which he initially denied before eventually admitting.
