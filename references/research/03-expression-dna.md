# W. Somerset Maugham: Expression DNA
## Stylistic Fingerprints, Voice Patterns, and Craft

---

*"The writer should seek his reward in the pleasure of his work and in release from the burden of his thought; and, indifferent to aught else, care nothing for praise or censure, failure or success."*
— The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter II

---

## 1. The Plain Prose Style: Maugham's Core Identity

Maugham's literary style was plain and functional; he disclaimed any pretence of being a prose stylist. Wikipedia's critical summary notes that "Maugham's plain prose style became known for its lucidity, but his reliance on clichés attracted adverse critical comment." This plainness was deliberate. As he wrote in his preface to The Painted Veil, he sought "ease in writing dialogue" through methodical study, not ornamental language.

Yet plainness is not simplicity. Maugham's prose is deceptively sophisticated — the surface reads effortlessly, but each sentence is calibrated for maximum precision. The lucidity that critics note is the result of careful craft, not carelessness.

**Evidence from The Moon and Sixpence (Chapter II):**

> "I forget who it was that recommended men for their soul's good to do each day two things they disliked: it was a wise man, and it is a precept that I have followed scrupulously; for every day I have got up and I have gone to bed."

This sentence is deceptively complex. It begins as an aside, becomes a joke, and ends with a dry punchline. The rhythm is perfect: the build-up of "every day I have got up and I have gone to bed" delivers the comedown with impeccable timing. The wit lies entirely in the understatement.

## 2. Famous Opening Lines: The Hook

Maugham's first sentences are deceptively simple but structurally engineered to create immediate tension. He rarely opens with action; instead, he opens with a philosophical observation or a disarming admission that pulls the reader into an intimate confessional space.

**The Moon and Sixpence — Opening:**

> "Charles Strickland lived obscurely. He made enemies rather than friends. It is not strange, then, that those who wrote of him should have eked out their scanty recollections with a lively fancy..."

The opening trilogy — "lived obscurely. He made enemies rather than friends" — establishes Strickland's outsider status in three words per clause. The transition to the biographers' myth-making immediately introduces the narrator's skeptical, ironic voice. The reader is positioned as someone who knows better than the legend-makers.

**Of Human Bondage — Opening:**
Philip is introduced through his physical difference (a club-foot) and his emotional response to leaving his nurse Emma. The opening grounds us in a child's perspective while the narrative voice maintains adult omniscience:

> "She began to cry, and Philip, though he did not quite understand, cried too. Emma was a tall, big-boned woman, with fair hair and large features. She came from Devonshire..."

The "though he did not quite understand" is devastating in its simplicity — the child cries without knowing why, but the narrator knows exactly why.

**The Painted Veil — Chapter i (Opening):**
> "She gave a startled cry. 'What's the matter?' he asked. Notwithstanding the darkness of the shuttered room he saw her face on a sudden distraught with terror. 'Some one just tried the door.'"

No preamble. The reader is dropped into the scene mid-crisis. The brevity of the dialogue — "What's the matter?" — contrasted with the elaborate description of her terror, creates immediate tension. This is Maugham's swiftness in action.

## 3. Sentence Structure: Short and Long, Controlled

Maugham alternates deliberately between very short declarative sentences and long, winding periods. The short sentences land like blows; the long ones create a hypnotic rhythm that lulls the reader into accepting their premises.

**Short sentence effect — from Of Human Bondage:**

> "Two years before Philip had been sent down to stay at the vicarage after an attack of chicken-pox; but there remained with him a recollection of an attic and a large garden rather than of his uncle and aunt."

This sentence does enormous work. It establishes Philip's outsider status, his distance from his new family, and his residual connection to a different life — all in one sentence. The semicolon creates a pivot from fact to interpretation, and the final phrase "rather than of his uncle and aunt" is a masterclass in saying something precise by saying what it is not.

**Long sentence effect — from The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter II:**

> "Sometimes a man survives a considerable time from an era in which he had his place into one which is strange to him, and then the curious are offered one of the most singular spectacles in the human comedy."

This sentence stretches the reader's attention across a long clause before landing on "the most singular spectacles in the human comedy." The comedy reference signals the narrator's detached, sardonic perspective on human vanity.

**From The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV — Physical description through controlled prose:**

> "She was a woman of thirty-seven, rather tall and plump, without being fat; she was not pretty, but her face was pleasing, chiefly, perhaps, on account of her kind brown eyes. Her skin was rather sallow. Her dark hair was elaborately dressed. She was the only woman of the three whose face was free of make-up, and by contrast with the others she seemed simple and unaffected."

Each observation is carefully ranked: height, figure, face, skin, hair, make-up. The "elaborately dressed" hair against "simple and unaffected" creates an ironic contrast that Maugham's narrator observes with dry amusement. Note the strategic use of "perhaps" — it introduces just enough uncertainty to seem honest rather than dogmatic.

## 4. Metaphor and Analogy: The Unforced Comparison

Maugham's metaphors are not decorative; they are argumentative. He uses them to reframe the reader's perception, often with dry humor.

**From The Moon and Sixpence — The critic as inquisitor:**

> "His heart warms to him when he can bring forward some example of cruelty or meanness, and he exults like an inquisitor at the auto da fé of an heretic when with some forgotten story he can confound the filial piety of the Rev. Robert Strickland."

The comparison of the critic Dr. Weitbrecht-Rotholz to an inquisitor at an auto da fé is devastating — it implicates the biographer in cruelty while seeming merely to describe his enthusiasm. The metaphor does the work of moral judgment without stating it directly.

**From the same passage — myth-making as romance:**

> "It is the protest of romance against the commonplace of life."

This single sentence encapsulates Maugham's philosophy of legend-making. It is not an explanation but an aphorism — the kind of neat phrasing that lodges in the memory. It typifies his ability to crystallize a complex observation into a single, quotable line.

**From "Mackintosh" (The Trembling of a Leaf) — The mosquitoes' humming:**

> "The mosquitoes flew about the net under which he slept in such numbers that their humming, pitiless and menacing, had the effect of a note, infinitely drawn out, played on a distant organ..."

The metaphor transforms an ordinary nuisance into something almost cosmically oppressive. "Infinitely drawn out" elevates the minor torment into a meditation on endurance and the relentless indifference of nature. Maugham takes a small sensory detail and expands it into something almost metaphysical.

## 5. Detached Narrator Voice: The Author as Bystander

Maugham's narrators are characteristically wry, self-aware, and reluctant to pass judgment. They participate in the story but maintain ironic distance, often acknowledging their own limitations or biases.

**From The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter II:**

> "But all this is by the way."

This is one of Maugham's signature digressions — the narrator stops the story to comment on his own storytelling. It is a deliberate breaching of narrative illusion, and it occurs repeatedly in Maugham's work. The effect is to make the reader feel they are being confided in rather than sold a narrative.

**Self-deprecating aside — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter II:**

> "There is in my nature a strain of asceticism, and I have subjected my flesh each week to a more severe mortification. I have never failed to read the Literary Supplement of The Times."

The wit lies in the escalation: asceticism, mortification, never failing to read the Literary Supplement. The irony is that reading book reviews is not conventionally a severe mortification, but the narrator presents it as such with deadpan seriousness. The joke is at his own expense.

**The narrator's meta-awareness — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV:**

> "I do not believe that there was in that genteel Bohemia an intensive culture of chastity, but I do not remember so crude a promiscuity as seems to be practised in the present day. We did not think it hypocritical to draw over our vagaries the curtain of a decent silence."

The phrase "the curtain of a decent silence" is Maugham at his most elegant. It captures an entire social attitude toward propriety in five words. The narrator observes the past with the detached perspective of someone who has moved through different eras and can see both sides.

## 6. Theme vs. Plot: The Surface and the Depth

Maugham's plots are deceptively simple — adultery, artistic ambition, spiritual seeking — but his thematic concerns run deep. He is interested in the gap between what people say and what they do, between the masks they wear and the selves they conceal.

**From The Painted Veil — Preface (Dante epigraph):**

> "'Siena mi fé; disfecemi Maremma' — Siena made me, Maremma unmade me."

The epigraph from Dante's Purgatorio establishes the novel's theme: a woman's life is shaped first by one force (her husband/Siena) and then unmade by another (the Maremma/her fate). Maugham writes: "I think this is the only novel I have written in which I started from a story rather than from a character." This admission is significant — it shows Maugham's awareness that his usual method is character-driven, not plot-driven.

**From The Painted Veil — Chapter ii:**
> "I say, could I have a brandy and soda?" "Yes, I'll ring." "I don't think it would hurt you by the look of things."

The exchange is comically bathetic — the two characters have just survived a terrifying ordeal, and their first concern is brandy. Maugham uses this trivial detail to ground the scene in human reality while the tension of the interrupted adultery lingers unspoken.

## 7. The "Crack in the Cake" Story Structure

Maugham's famous structural device — the story-within-a-story, or what he called the "crack in the cake" — involves embedding a narrative within a frame. The frame narrator encounters a character or situation and then tells that character's story, often with a twist that reframes the frame.

**Evidence from The Moon and Sixpence:** The frame narrator is an anonymous "I" who meets Charles Strickland in London society, travels to Paris, and eventually hears about Strickland's life in Tahiti from those who knew him there. The biography-within-the-novel (the Rev. Robert Strickland's official biography and Dr. Weitbrecht-Rotholz's counter-biography) creates multiple layers of narrative distance, so the reader never gets Strickland's story directly but always filtered through someone's interpretation.

**Evidence from "Mackintosh" (The Trembling of a Leaf):** The story opens with the narrator describing the Pacific voyage, then introduces Mackintosh as the subject of the administrator Walker's portrait. Walker tells stories about himself with "a rough cordiality" that the narrator observes with growing coldness. The "crack" here is between Walker's self-mythologizing and the narrator's accumulating evidence of his vulgarity.

**Maugham's own description of his method (from The Painted Veil preface):**

> "I think this is the only novel I have written in which I started from a story rather than from a character. It is difficult to explain the relation between character and plot. You cannot very well think of a character in the void; the moment you think of him, you think of him in some situation, doing something."

This is Maugham the craftsman speaking — he is self-aware about the relationship between character and plot, and he understands that the "crack in the cake" is a way of managing the distance between the story as told and the story as lived.

## 8. Language Accessible to Common Readers

Maugham deliberately wrote for a wide audience. He rejected the experimental prose of modernism and maintained a traditional narrative approach that did not challenge the reader's expectations.

**Evidence — Wikipedia summarizes the critical consensus:**

> "Maugham's literary style was plain and functional; he disclaimed any pretence of being a prose stylist."

This plainness was strategic. Maugham believed that story was paramount, and he refused to let style get in the way of narrative momentum. He was skeptical of literary pretension — a stance he articulates repeatedly through his narrator's mockery of literary lion-hunters and affected Bohemians.

**From The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV:**

> "The dining-room was in the good taste of the period. It was very severe. There was a high dado of white wood and a green paper on which were etchings by Whistler in neat black frames. The green curtains with their peacock design, hung in straight lines, and the green carpet, in the pattern of which pale rabbits frolicked among leafy trees, suggested the influence of William Morris. There was blue delft on the chimney-piece. At that time there must have been five hundred dining-rooms in London decorated in exactly the same manner. It was chaste, artistic, and dull."

The description is impeccably clear, with no unusual vocabulary or syntax. The final three-word sentence — "It was chaste, artistic, and dull" — is the devastating verdict. The plainness of the prose mirrors the tastelessness of the décor it describes.

## 9. Dry Wit and Irony

Maugham's humor is understated, precise, and often directed at institutions, pretensions, and the gap between social performance and reality. It is never broad or slapstick; it operates through observation, understatement, and the strategic placement of a telling detail.

**The satire of literary biography — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter I:**

> "The son has drawn the portrait of an excellent husband and father, a man of kindly temper, industrious habits, and moral disposition. The modern clergyman has acquired in his study of the science which I believe is called exegesis an astonishing facility for explaining things away..."

The wit here is multi-layered: the narrator pretends not to know the word "exegesis" (a knowing fake ignorance) while simultaneously skewering the clergyman's talent for "explaining things away." The phrase "I believe" performs mock-modesty while delivering the sharpest possible critique.

**The letter forgery comedy — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter I:**

> "if Charles Strickland left a laundry bill unpaid it will be given you in extenso, and if he forebore to return a borrowed half-crown no detail of the transaction will be omitted."

"In extenso" (in full) applied to a laundry bill is a mock-heroic touch. The image of Dr. Weitbrecht-Rotholz poring over Strickland's unpaid debts with the same scholarly intensity as art-historical analysis is one of Maugham's finest comic passages.

**The dinner party description — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV:**

> "The men were seldom eccentric in appearance. They tried to look as little like authors as possible. They wished to be taken for men of the world, and could have passed anywhere for the managing clerks of a city firm."

The irony is that these writers who want to seem conventional are, in Maugham's eyes, the truly eccentric ones. The deadpan observation — "They always seemed a little tired" — carries the weight of a sociological insight.

## 10. Physical Details That Reveal Character

Maugham is a master of the revealing physical detail — the small, precise observation that implies a whole psychology.

**Walker from "Mackintosh" (The Trembling of a Leaf):**

> "He was a little man, considerably less than of middle height, and enormously stout; he had a large, fleshy face, clean-shaven, with the cheeks hanging on each side in great dew-laps, and three vast chins; his small features were all dissolved in fat; and, but for a crescent of white hair at the back of his head, he was completely bald. He reminded you of Mr Pickwick."

The catalogue of physical details — dwarfed height, enormous stoutness, dew-laps, chins, dissolved features — builds a portrait of a man whose body has escaped his control. The Mr. Pickwick comparison simultaneously humanizes and ridicules. The narrator observes without editorializing, but the physical portrait damns.

**Mackintosh (the same story):**

> "Mackintosh was an ugly man, with ungainly gestures, a tall thin fellow, with a narrow chest and bowed shoulders. He had sallow, sunken cheeks, and his eyes were large and sombre."

The contrast with Walker is deliberate: tall/thin vs. little/stout; sallow vs. florid; bowed shoulders vs. heavy tread. Maugham uses physical contrast as characterization — these two men are body-based opposites, and their personalities follow from their physiques.

**Mrs. Strickland's face — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV:**

> "She was a woman of thirty-seven, rather tall and plump, without being fat; she was not pretty, but her face was pleasing, chiefly, perhaps, on account of her kind brown eyes."

"Chiefly, perhaps" — the double qualifier is masterful. Maugham refuses to commit to a full compliment; instead, he creates the impression of honest, observant narration. The "kind brown eyes" humanize her without idealizing her.

## 11. The Maugham Effect: Force, Swiftness, and Dramatic Leaps

Critics have noted what is variously called "The Maugham Effect" or simply Maugham's ability to make the reader turn pages. His force comes from several combined techniques:

**a) Sudden scene shifts:** Maugham drops the reader into a scene without warning, as in The Painted Veil's opening chapter, where Kitty and her lover are interrupted mid-adultery.

**b) Rapid moral reversals:** In The Moon and Sixpence, the reader's sympathy for Strickland shifts repeatedly — from artist to selfish deserter to visionary to tragic hero — as Maugham layers competing perspectives.

**c) Compressed climaxes:** The Moon and Sixpence climaxes with Strickland's death and the burning of his great murals — a dramatic reversal that Maugham describes with restrained intensity:

> "He had gone to the house that was his own... He did not want peace. He wanted struggle."

**d) The devastating final line:** Maugham frequently ends chapters or sections with a single sentence that reframes everything before it. The narrator's constant digressions are justified by the final, precise observation that makes the reader see the preceding material in a new light.

**Evidence — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter IV closing:**

> "When we had done discussing the merits of the latest book, it was natural to wonder how many copies had been sold, what advance the author had received, and how much he was likely to make out of it."

The shift from aesthetic discussion to commercial calculation is presented as "natural" — and that word, in Maugham's ironic context, is the sharpest possible critique of literary Bohemia.

**Evidence — The Painted Veil, Chapter i:**

> "They stood facing the windows that led out on the verandah. They were shuttered and the shutters were bolted. They saw the white china knob of the handle slowly turn."

The scene builds through short, declarative sentences. The reader watches the knob turn with the characters — suspended in suspense. The silence, the stealth, the "ghastliness of the supernatural" — all create a pressure that Maugham releases only gradually.

## 12. The Ironic Detachment as Moral Position

Maugham's detachment is not emotional absence but moral discipline. He refuses to tell the reader what to think; he shows, and he lets the irony do the work of judgment.

**The narrator's philosophy — The Moon and Sixpence, Chapter II:**

> "The new evangel was old when Nineveh reared her greatness to the sky. These gallant words which seem so novel to those that speak them were said in accents scarcely changed a hundred times before. The pendulum swings backwards and forwards. The circle is ever travelled anew."

This is Maugham's philosophy of history: cyclical, skeptical of novelty, amused by human pretension. The prose is epigrammatic — each sentence is self-contained and quotable. The tone is neither cynical nor optimistic; it is simply, firmly realistic about human nature.

**From the same chapter:**

> "I do not like them. I am on the shelf. I will continue to write moral stories in rhymed couplets."

The narrator places himself outside the contemporary literary fashion with a self-deprecating shrug that is also a declaration of independence. The humor is dry, the independence is real.

## Summary: The Maugham Fingerprint

| Technique | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Plain prose** | "Plain and functional"; lucidity over ornament |
| **First-sentence hooks** | "Charles Strickland lived obscurely"; "She gave a startled cry" |
| **Short/long alternation** | Staccato declaratives followed by long reflective periods |
| **Revealing details** | Walker's dew-laps; Mrs. Strickland's "kind brown eyes" |
| **Dry wit** | "I have subjected my flesh each week to a more severe mortification" |
| **Detached narrator** | "But all this is by the way"; self-aware digressions |
| **Irony over statement** | Lets the evidence condemn; rarely pronounces judgment |
| **"Crack in the cake"** | Frame narrative; Strickland filtered through biographers |
| **Accessible language** | No lexical pretension; vocabulary of common speech |
| **Force and swiftness** | Sudden scene shifts; compressed climaxes; devastating final lines |
| **Thematic depth** | Plots are simple; themes (duty, desire, authenticity) are not |
| **Physical characterization** | Body as destiny; every physical detail implies psychology |
