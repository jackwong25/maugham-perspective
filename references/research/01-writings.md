# W. Somerset Maugham: Books and Systematic Writings on Writing/Craft

## Overview
W. Somerset Maugham (1874–1965) was an English novelist, playwright, and short-story writer whose work is characterized by a clear unadorned style, cosmopolitan settings, and a shrewd understanding of human nature. His four central novels are Of Human Bondage (1915), The Moon and Sixpence (1919), Cakes and Ale (1930), and The Razor's Edge (1944). He published extensively and is regarded as a master of the short story form. His systematic writings on craft appear primarily in *The Summing Up* (1938) and *A Writer's Notebook* (1949).

## I. MAJOR NOVELS AND CORE THEMES

### Of Human Bondage (1915)
- **Source**: Wikipedia, Britannica
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own novel)
- **Themes**: A semi-autobiographical novel following Philip Carey, a young medical student with a club foot (equated with Maugham's stammer or homosexuality), from childhood through his painful progress toward maturity. The novel explores the relationship between intellect and passion, the search for meaning, and the痛苦 of self-discovery.
- **Key quotes**: 
  - "I am in contact with what I most wanted, life in the raw" (Maugham on his medical training experiences that informed the novel)
  - The hero suffers loss of mother, breakup of family home, emotionally straitened upbringing by elderly relatives
- **Note**: Regarded as his masterpiece by modern critics; autobiographical elements include the physical disability and orphanhood

### The Moon and Sixpence (1919)
- **Source**: Wikipedia, Britannica  
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own novel)
- **Themes**: Based loosely on Paul Gauguin's life—an artist abandons conventional life to pursue painting. Explores the tension between social obligation and artistic compulsion, the nature of artistic genius, and the cost of following one's daemon.
- **Key quotes**: 
  - "I am an artist and you are right, you're not mad, I am a great artist and I know it. It's because I know it that I have endured such sufferings."
  - "Maugham was fascinated by the impact of the arrival of modernism from Europe on an insular British consciousness"
- **Note**: Satire of Edwardian mores combined with Gauguin biography

### The Razor's Edge (1944)
- **Source**: Wikipedia, Britannica
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own novel)
- **Themes**: Story of a young American war veteran who rejects conventional life to seek spiritual enlightenment. Explores the conflict between material success and spiritual quest, the search for transcendent meaning.
- **Note**: Final major novel; explores philosophical themes more explicitly than his earlier works

### Cakes and Ale (1930)
- **Source**: Britannica
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own novel)
- **Themes**: Story of a famous novelist (thought to contain caricatures of Thomas Hardy and Hugh Walpole); examines literary reputation, the nature of success, and the relationship between artist and society.
- **Note**: Title phrase comes from Shakespeare's "Twelfth Night": "Make me eat the cake and ale."

### The Painted Veil (1925)
- **Source**: Britannica
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own novel)
- **Themes**: Set in colonial Hong Kong and China; explores adultery, redemption, and the moral transformation of the protagonist.
- **Note**: One of his most frequently adapted novels

## II. SHORT STORY COLLECTIONS

### General Characteristics
- **Source**: Britannica, Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Secondary (analysis of his published works)
- **Structure**: Known for economy and suspense; Maugham handles plot in the manner of Guy de Maupassant
- **Notable collections**:
  - *The Trembling of a Leaf* (1921)
  - *The Casuarina Tree* (1926) — includes "Rain"
  - *Ashenden* (1928) — spy stories drawn from WWI experience
  - *Ah King* (1933)
  - *The Mixture as Before* (1940)
  - *Creatures of Circumstance* (1947)
- **Key observation**: Many portray conflict of Europeans in alien surroundings; distinguished by economy and suspense

### "Rain" (from The Casuarina Tree, 1926)
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own story)
- **Note**: Adapted into the play "Miss Sadie Thompson"; the biggest theatrical success of Maugham's career was an adaptation by others of "Rain," which opened on Broadway in 1921 and ran for 648 performances

## III. SYSTEMATIC WRITINGS ON CRAFT

### The Summing Up (1938)
- **Source**: Wikipedia ("The Summing Up" page)
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself wrote)
- **Nature**: Literary memoir written when Maugham was 64 years old, covering his life from 1890 to 1938
- **Content**: Covers his childhood, initial success in theater, transition from theater to fiction writing, travel, and philosophy
- **Key quote**: "It is a small book filled with memorable quotes"
- **Themes addressed**: His philosophy of life as "a resigned atheism and a certain skepticism about the extent of man's innate goodness and intelligence; it is this that gives his work its astringent cynicism"

### A Writer's Notebook (1949)
- **Source**: Wikipedia, Britannica
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself wrote)
- **Nature**: Collection of his observations about writing, life, and craft
- **Themes**: Philosophy of life, resigned atheism, skepticism about human goodness and intelligence; astringent cynicism
- **Note**: Published posthumously or near end of his career; contains raw observations about the writing craft

### Ten Novels and Their Authors (1954)
- **Source**: Wikipedia (in list of works)
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself wrote)
- **Nature**: Critical study of ten novels and their authors
- **Note**: Non-fiction work examining the craft of fiction through analysis of other novelists

### On a Chinese Screen (1922)
- **Source**: Wikipedia (in list of works)
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself wrote)
- **Nature**: Travel/writing observations from his travels in Asia

## IV. QUOTES ON CRAFT AND FICTION (from various sources)

### On Style
- **Source**: Wikipedia (Writing Style section)
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself wrote/observed)
- **Key quote**: "Words have weight, sound, and appearance; it is only by considering these that you can write a sentence that is good to look at and good to listen to."
- **Attributed to**: J.C. Trewell quoting Maugham's observation on dialogue design

### On Writing Process
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own statement)
- **Key quote**: "I took to it [writing] as a duck takes to water."

### On Dialogue
- **Source**: Wikipedia (J.C. Trewin quote)
- **Credibility**: Secondary (critic's analysis of Maugham's dialogue)
- **Key observation**: "His dialogue, unlike that of many of his contemporaries, is designed to be spoken... Maugham does not write elaborately visual prose"

### On Drama vs. Fiction
- **Source**: Wikipedia (Maugham quote on leaving theater)
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham himself said)
- **Key quote**: "I am done with playwriting... I cannot tell you how I loathe the theatre... After one has got over the glamour of the stage and the excitement, I do not myself think the theatre has much to offer the writer compared with the other mediums in which he has complete independence and need consider no one."

## V. RECURRING THEMES (appearing in 3+ works)

### 1. The Quest for Meaning/Spiritual Seeking
- **Works**: Of Human Bondage, The Razor's Edge, The Moon and Sixpence
- **Credibility**: Primary
- **Note**: Characters seek transcendence through art, spirituality, or intellectual pursuits

### 2. The Conflict Between Convention and Individual Impulse
- **Works**: The Moon and Sixpence, The Razor's Edge, Cakes and Ale, Of Human Bondage
- **Credibility**: Primary
- **Note**: Artists and individuals struggle against societal expectations

### 3. The Cost of Artistic Commitment
- **Works**: The Moon and Sixpence, Cakes and Ale, The Razor's Edge
- **Credibility**: Primary
- **Note**: Art demands sacrifice; artists must choose between worldly success and authentic creation

### 4. Europeans in Alien Environments
- **Works**: The Painted Veil, The Moon and Sixpence, numerous short stories
- **Credibility**: Primary
- **Note**: His stories frequently portray Europeans confronting alien cultures that provoke strong emotions

### 5. Cynicism and Resigned Atheism
- **Works**: The Summing Up, A Writer's Notebook, and the astringent tone throughout his fiction
- **Credibility**: Primary
- **Note**: Britannica identifies this as his core philosophical stance

## VII. VIEWS ON WHAT MAKES GOOD FICTION

### From Wikipedia/Writing Style section:
- **Plain Prose Style**: Maugham's literary style was plain and functional; he disclaimed any pretence of being a prose stylist
- **Influence**: Followed no master but acknowledged Guy de Maupassant as an early influence
- **Affinities**: British writers with stylistic affinities include Jonathan Swift, William Hazlitt, John Dryden, and John Henry Newman — "all practitioners of precise prose"

### "The Maugham Effect" (J.D. Scott in The Spectator):
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Secondary (critical analysis)
- **Key quote**: "This quality is one of force, of swiftness, of the dramatic leap"
- **Note**: Style more effective in narrative than in suggestion and nuance

### On Clichés:
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Secondary (critical observation)
- **Note**: H.E. Bates praised many of Maugham's attributes but objected to frequent reliance on clichéd phrases. George Lyttelton commented that Maugham "purchases a beautiful lucidity at the cost of numberless clichés"

### On Entertainment vs. Art:
- **From "The Creative Impulse" (1926 short story):
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Primary (Maugham's own fiction making fun of self-conscious stylists)
- **Key quote**: "It was indeed a scandal that so distinguished an author, with an imagination so delicate and a style so exquisite, should remain neglected of the vulgar"

### Critical Reception:
- **Source**: Wikipedia
- **Credibility**: Secondary
- **Note**: "He is never boring or clumsy, he never gives a false impression; he is never shocking; but this very diplomatic polish makes impossible for him any of those sudden transcendent flashes of passion and beauty which less competent novelists occasionally attain."

## VIII. CONTRADICTIONS FOUND

No significant contradictions were found in the sources consulted. However, note:
1. Maugham abandoned theater (said he "loathed" it) yet achieved his greatest commercial success there before becoming a novelist
2. He claimed to disclaim prose styling while being criticized for relying on clichés to achieve a casual, "well-bred" tone
