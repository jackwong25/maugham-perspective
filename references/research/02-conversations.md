# Maugham's Conversations, Interviews, and Dialogue Patterns

## Research Findings

### 1. The Paris Review Interview (1963) — "The Art of Fiction No. 26"

**Source**: The Paris Review, Summer 1963 (Issue 28)
**Credibility**: A+ (Primary source — famous literary interview series)

While direct access to the archived interview was blocked, secondary sources confirm this interview is one of the most important extended dialogues with Maugham. The Art of Fiction series typically features authors discussing their craft, philosophy, and approach to writing in depth.

Key characteristics of Maugham's interview style documented elsewhere:
- He was known for his direct, unadorned manner of speaking — matching his prose style
- He avoided pretension and spoke practically about the writer's craft
- He demonstrated the skeptical, detached outlook that characterized his fiction

**Finding**: Maugham approached interviews the way he approached writing — with clarity, directness, and an absence of affectation. He did not grandstand or offer elaborate theories.

---

### 2. Maugham's "The Summing Up" (1938) as Extended Self-Dialogue

**Source**: W. Somerset Maugham, *The Summing Up* (1938), Doubleday
**Credibility**: A+ (Primary source — Maugham's own autobiographical reflections)

This book is essentially Maugham in conversation with himself — a sustained meditation on his philosophy of life, art, and writing. Unlike typical memoirs, it is philosophical and unsentimental.

Notable self-reflective passages documented in secondary sources:

> "I have no illusions about my literary position. There are but two important critics in my own country who have troubled to take me seriously and when clever young men write essays about contemporary fiction they never think of considering me. I do not resent it. It is very natural."

**Finding**: Maugham demonstrated unusual frankness about his perceived limitations. He acknowledged writing from observation rather than great imagination: "the greatest writers can see through a brick wall — my vision is not so penetrating."

This self-assessment shows a man comfortable with his own capacities, neither false modesty nor vanity.

### 3. "A Writer's Notebook" (1949) as Ongoing Self-Dialogue

**Source**: W. Somerset Maugham, *A Writer's Notebook* (1949), Heinemann
**Credibility**: A+ (Primary source)

This notebook contains Maugham's observations, opinions, and reflections gathered over decades. It reveals:

**How he discussed other writers:**

On D.H. Lawrence (documented from secondary sources):
- Maugham acknowledged Lawrence's intensity but found his prose overwrought at times
- He respected Lawrence's passion but was put off by his didacticism
- Maugham believed Lawrence's message sometimes overwhelmed his art
- The relationship was complicated — Lawrence saw Maugham as representative of everything he opposed in English society

On T.S. Eliot:
- Maugham admired Eliot's intellectual rigor but found his poetry difficult
- He respected Eliot's influence on modern literature
- He remained somewhat outside modernist literary circles despite occasional contact

On Thomas Hardy:
- In *Cakes and Ale* (1930), Maugham created a character (Edward Drummond) clearly modeled on Hardy
- He also satirized Hugh Walpole as Alroy Kear — and initially denied it
- **Crucially**: Maugham eventually admitted the denial was a lie

**Finding**: Maugham could be sharp and satirical about contemporaries, but also honest about his own deceptions. He showed a capacity for self-awareness about his own pettiness.

### 4. BBC Television Appearances — Maugham as On-Screen Presence

**Source**: BBC Genome (BBC archival records); Wikipedia citing BBC records
**Credibility**: A (Primary archival source)

Maugham appeared on BBC television introducing adaptations of his own works:
- The *Somerset Maugham Theater* series brought his stories to millions
- A reviewer noted he enjoyed "tremendous popularity... and has won for him an audience of millions of enthusiastic fans"
- He appeared in on-screen introductions for film adaptations (*Quartet* 1948, *Trio* 1950, *Encore* 1951)

**Finding**: Maugham was willing to present himself publicly, but always in the role of storyteller rather than personality. He maintained a dignified distance, preferring to let the work speak for itself.

### 5. Responses to Difficult Questions — The Hugh Walpole Incident

**Source**: Wikipedia (citing Hastings, *Maugham*); multiple biographical sources
**Credibility**: A (Well-documented biographical incident)

When asked about *Cakes and Ale* and whether Alroy Kear was based on Hugh Walpole:
- Maugham initially denied it
- Few believed him
- He **eventually admitted it was a lie**

**Finding**: Maugham could be caught in deceptions and would eventually admit them, though sometimes tardily. This suggests a complex relationship with truth — he could dissemble when it suited him, but eventually honored honesty over face-saving.

### 6. Maugham on His Own Weaknesses and Regrets

**Source**: Wikipedia; *The Summing Up*; biographical sources
**Credibility**: A (Well-documented personal admissions)

**Homosexuality and marriage regrets:**
- Maugham described his attempt to be heterosexual as "the greatest mistake in his life"
- He told his nephew Robin: "I tried to persuade myself that I was three-quarters normal and that only a quarter of me was queer — whereas really it was the other way round"
- He married Syria Wellcome in 1917, a marriage that lasted 12 years but was troubled

**Artistic limitations:**
- He acknowledged lacking "any great powers of imagination"
- He said he wrote about what he saw, and while he saw more than most, "the greatest writers can see through a brick wall — my vision is not so penetrating"
- He accepted critical assessments of his work as competent but not transcendent

**Finding**: Maugham demonstrated unusual candor about personal weaknesses. He did not rewrite history to flatter himself. This directness extended to his view of his own abilities.

### 7. Maugham's Views on Other Writers — Documented Opinions

**Source**: Wikipedia; *The Summing Up*; *A Writer's Notebook*; various biographical sources
**Credibility**: A (Multiple documented sources)

**D.H. Lawrence**:
- Maugham acknowledged Lawrence's gifts but found him excessive
- He saw Lawrence as philosophically overbearing
- Lawrence's moralistic certainty was the opposite of Maugham's skepticism

**T.S. Eliot**:
- Respected without being swept away
- Found the poetry challenging but admired the intellect

**Thomas Hardy**:
- Clearly admired, evidenced by the thinly-veiled character in *Cakes and Ale*
- Yet also satirical — showing complicated feelings

**Hugh Walpole**:
- Maugham's mockery of Walpole in *Cakes and Ale* was apparently motivated by personal grievance ("a stolen boyfriend, an unrequited love and an old canker of jealousy" — per Hastings)
- This reveals that Maugham's literary judgments could be colored by personal animus

**Evelyn Waugh**:
- Waugh admired Maugham's craft while noting its limitations
- Said Maugham's "diplomatic polish makes impossible for him any of those sudden transcendent flashes of passion and beauty"

**Edmund Wilson**:
- Wilson called Maugham second-rate and disappointing
- Wilson later admitted he hadn't actually read Maugham's major works
- Maugham reportedly did not resent Wilson's dismissal

**Finding**: Maugham's views on other writers were generally balanced — he could acknowledge gifts even in writers he didn't admire, and his respect was earned through craft rather than grand ambition. His opinions were sometimes Personal.

### 8. Maugham's Dialogue Style — Evidence from His Writing

**Source**: Wikipedia (citing J.C. Trewin and other critics)
**Credibility**: A (Documented critical analysis)

Critic J.C. Trewin noted Maugham's dialogue was "designed to be spoken" — naturalistic rather than stylized. Maugham himself observed:

> "Words have weight, sound, and appearance; it is only by considering these that you can write a sentence that is good to look at and good to listen to."

Maugham's prose style in dialogue sought to replicate "the conversation of a well-bred man" — though critics noted he relied on clichés ("got along like a house afire," "didn't care a row of pins for each other").

**Finding**: Maugham's approach to dialogue was conscious and crafted. He wanted naturalism, clarity, and speakability. He was attentive to the sound of language even in interview contexts.

---

### 9. Dramatic Shifts in Opinion — The Walpole Lie

**Source**: Wikipedia (citing multiple biographical sources)
**Credibility**: A (Well-documented case of shifting position)

Maugham's denial of the Walpole caricature, followed by eventual admission, represents a clear case of opinion shift under pressure.

Other documented shifts:
- Early enthusiasm for the theatre, later disdain ("I cannot tell you how I loathe the theatre")
- Initial denial of *Cakes and Ale*'s targets, later admissions
- Changing relationship with literary establishment over time

**Finding**: Maugham was not rigid in his positions. He could reverse himself, particularly when confronted with evidence. He showed pragmatic flexibility.

## Summary of Interview/Dialogue Characteristics

Based on all evidence, Maugham's approach to conversation and interview:

1. **Direct and unpretentious**: He matched his prose style in speech — clear, unadorned, accessible

2. **Skeptical and detached**: He approached questions with a cynic's wariness, rarely offering emotional or enthusiastic responses

3. **Frank about limitations**: He honestly assessed his own weaknesses and artistic limits

4. **Sharp-tongued when pressed**: He could be satirical about contemporaries, especially when personal grievances were involved

5. **Willing to admit error**: When caught in deceptions, he eventually confessed — showing integrity beneath the social diplomat's mask

6. **Professional and controlled**: He maintained authorial dignity in public appearances, sticking to the work rather than cultivating personality

7. **Occasionally combative**: He did not suffer fools gladly and could be dismissive of questions he deemed unworthy
