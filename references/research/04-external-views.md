# External Views of William Somerset Maugham: Critical Assessments, Controversies, and Debates

## Overview

William Somerset Maugham (1874-1965) presents one of the most striking paradoxes in twentieth-century English literature: a writer who was simultaneously enormously popular with the reading public, highly influential on subsequent generations of writers, yet persistently dismissed or condescended to by the literary establishment and highbrow critics. This document surveys the major critical assessments of Maugham's work, the controversies surrounding specific works, and the ongoing debate about his place in the literary canon.

---

## 1. Theodore Dreiser's Intervention and the Problem of Initial Reception

Maugham's 1915 novel *Of Human Bondage* received mixed-to-hostile initial reviews. The *New York World* dismissed Philip Carey's romantic obsession as "the sentimental servitude of a poor fool." Many British reviewers were similarly unenthusiastic. The tide turned when the American novelist and critic **Theodore Dreiser** publicly championed the book, calling Maugham a great artist and the novel a work of genius, "of the utmost importance, comparable to a Beethoven symphony." Bryan Connon observed in the *Oxford Dictionary of National Biography* that after Dreiser's endorsement, "it seemed that Maugham could not fail, and the public eagerly bought his novels and volumes of his carefully crafted short stories." Dreiser's early support stands as one of the most significant critical endorsements in Maugham's career, and illustrates how establishment British criticism often followed rather than led in appreciating Maugham's work.

---

## 2. The "Crack in the Cake" Story Structure

One persistent structural criticism of Maugham's fiction concerns what might be called the **"crack in the cake"** approach to story construction—particularly visible in the *Ashenden* spy stories and in *The Moon and Sixpence*. The technique involves beginning a narrative with knowledge of an ending (the protagonist is already dead before *The Moon and Sixpence* opens), then attempting to piece together the story retrospectively. Critics have argued this creates a fundamental structural tension: the reader knows the outcome before the investigation begins, which can flatten dramatic suspense.

In *The Moon and Sixpence*, the protagonist Charles Strickland is already dead when the novel opens; the narrator Ashenden-like figure pieces together Strickland's story, particularly his final Tahitian years. The unusual structure has been read as both a strength (allowing ironic distance) and a weakness (undermining dramatic momentum). Amy Dickson describes the novel as "a satire of Edwardian mores and a Gauguin biography," noting Maugham's fascination with the emergence of the modernist artist-genius. The structure, in this view, mirrors the impossibility of fully knowing such a figure after the fact.

The *Ashenden* collection (1928) employs a related technique: stories are narrated by a writer-recruit who pieces together intelligence operations, often with moral ambiguity about the nature of the information gathered. This retrospective, investigative mode has been both praised (as influencing John le Carré and Ian Fleming) and criticized as fundamentally static.

## 3. "Craftsman but Not Artist": The Competence Critique

The most persistent criticism of Maugham is that he was a skilled craftsman—a superb technician of plot and prose—but lacked the imaginative vision and stylistic innovation of truly major writers. This critique takes many forms:

**Lee Wilson Dodd** wrote: "Mr Maugham knows how to plan a story and carry it through. Competence is the word. His style is without a trace of imaginative beauty."

**L.A.G. Strong** acknowledged Maugham's craftsmanship but memorably described his writing as having an effect "like that of music expertly played in an expensive restaurant at dinner"—technically accomplished background entertainment rather than transcendent art.

**Lytton Strachey** disparaged one of Maugham's books as **"Class II, Division I"**—a phrase that crystallized the literary establishment's view of Maugham's proper station.

**Evelyn Waugh**, though an admirer, articulated the paradox with precision: Maugham's disciplined writing possessed "brilliant technical dexterity," but "this very diplomatic polish makes impossible for him any of those sudden transcendent flashes of passion and beauty which less competent novelists occasionally attain."

**Edmund Wilson** insisted Maugham was second-rate and "disappointing." Notably, Wilson later admitted he had not actually read *Of Human Bondage*, *Cakes and Ale*, or *The Razor's Edge*—a revealing admission about the confidence of dismissal even from sophisticated critics. This contradiction is itself significant: Maugham's critical reputation was often held by critics who had not engaged deeply with his work.

**Philip Holden** (2006) captured the paradox: Maugham "outsold, and outlived, contemporaries such as James Joyce, Virginia Woolf and D.H. Lawrence," but "could not match them in terms of stylistic innovation or thematic complexity." Yet Holden also noted Maugham's important influence on Coward, Lawrence, Kingsley Amis, Graham Greene, Christopher Isherwood, V.S. Naipaul, George Orwell, Raymond Chandler, Ian Fleming, and John le Carré.

**Don Adams** (2016) observed: "The gist of the criticism of Maugham's fiction, that it lacks psychological and emotional profundity, is remarkably consistent throughout the decades."

### The Famed "Second-Rater" Quote: A Fact-Check

A frequently cited quote—in which Maugham supposedly said "I know just where I stand—in the very first row of the second-raters"—has been repeated in press articles, biographies, and dictionaries of quotations. However, as Daniel Blackburn and Alexander Arsov demonstrated in a 2016 article in *English Literature in Transition 1880-1920*, **this phrase does not appear in Maugham's work and there is no known evidence that he ever used it.** Ted Morgan's 1980 biography of Maugham appears to be the source of this misattribution. The apocryphal quote has taken on a life of its own, illustrating how the "second-rater" narrative was so congenial to critics that they accepted and repeated a quotation without verification.

## 4. Style Criticism: Clichés and Lucidity

Maugham's prose style attracted consistent critical attention—often for contradictory reasons.

Maugham's style was deliberately plain and functional. He disclaimed any pretension to being a prose stylist. Kenneth Funsten (1981) traced his stylistic affinities to Jonathan Swift, William Hazlitt, John Dryden, and John Henry Newman—all "practitioners of precise prose." The *Oxford Dictionary of Quotations* cites Maugham only ten times, compared with nearly a hundred for his contemporary Bernard Shaw.

**H.E. Bates** praised many of Maugham's attributes but objected to his "frequent reliance on clichéd phrases."

**George Lyttelton** commented that Maugham "purchases a beautiful lucidity at the cost of numberless clichés," but ranked his lucidity second only to Shaw's.

Maugham's use of clichéd narrator's-voice expressions drew particular fire. Morgan catalogs phrases like: characters "got along like a house afire," "didn't care a row of pins for each other," exchanged "sardonic grins" and "disparaging glances." A person was "as clever as a bagful of monkeys," beauty "took your breath away," a friend was "a damned good sort," a villain was "an unmitigated scoundrel," a bore "talked your head off," and the hero's heart "beat nineteen to the dozen."

**J.C. Trewin** defended Maugham differently, noting that his dialogue was "designed to be spoken" and that Maugham understood "Words have weight, sound, and appearance; it is only by considering these that you can write a sentence that is good to look at and good to listen to."

The critique of Maugham's style thus presents a genuine tension: his clarity and directness were praised as virtues, but his reliance on conventional phrases undermined the very clarity he sought. The contradiction is real—Maugham achieved remarkable transparency of narrative while using language that was, in itself, unremarkable.

## 5. The *Cakes and Ale* Controversy and the Hugh Walpole Caricature

No episode in Maugham's career illustrates the dangers of his satirical pen better than the *Cakes and Ale* (1930) affair. The novel is a satirical portrait of the London literary world, with Edward Driffield widely read as a (disrespectful) portrait of Thomas Hardy, who had died in 1928, and Alroy Kear as a caricature of **Hugh Walpole**.

Maugham initially denied the Walpole connection: "I certainly never intended Alroy Kear to be a portrait of you. He is made up of a dozen people and the greater part of him is myself." However, in a 1950 Modern Library introduction, Maugham admitted that Walpole was indeed the inspiration for Kear—though he continued to deny the Hardy connection. Few had believed his original denial, and the controversy damaged his reputation among literary peers.

Selina Hastings quotes a contemporary's view that Kear was Maugham's "revenge on Walpole for 'a stolen boyfriend, an unrequited love and an old canker of jealousy.'" Raphael describes *Cakes and Ale* as "an elegant piece of literary malice." Myrick Land argues in *The Fine Art of Literary Mayhem* that the novel "ruined the last 11 years of Walpole's life and destroyed his reputation as a writer."

The controversy illustrates several tensions in Maugham's character: his willingness to use literary portraiture for personal settling of scores, his initial dishonesty about it, and the way his satirical gifts made him enemies. It also damaged his standing with the literary establishment, who saw in the affair both vindictiveness and dishonesty.

## 6. Maugham's Relationship with the Literary Establishment

Maugham was never comfortably inside the literary establishment, and the feeling was mutual. He wrote in *The Summing Up* (1938):

> "I have no illusions about my literary position. There are but two important critics in my own country who have trouble to take me seriously and when clever young men write essays about contemporary fiction they never think of considering me. I do not resent it. It is very natural."

The "two important critics" were probably **Desmond MacCarthy** and **Raymond Mortimer**—MacCarthy who praised the short stories, tracing their roots in French naturalism, and Mortimer who reviewed Maugham's books carefully and favorably in the *New Statesman*. Maugham was not being falsely modest; he accurately identified his marginal position in highbrow critical discourse.

**Cyril Connolly**—a rising younger critic—praised Maugham for his lucidity and called him "the last of the great professional writers," but Connolly's approval was not shared by all his contemporaries. Connolly's remark is notable: it positions Maugham as the end of a tradition—the last professional rather than a pioneer.

**Virginia Woolf** was "friendly though a little patronising," according to Wikipedia's sources. The patronization is significant: even those who knew Maugham socially and recognized his abilities could not quite place him in the first rank.

**Lytton Strachey's** "Class II, Division I" verdict captures the establishment view: Maugham was talented, perhaps, but not among the truly great.

Marking Maugham's eightieth birthday, *The New York Times* offered a notably balanced assessment: Maugham had "outlived his contemporaries" including Shaw, Conrad, Wells, James, Bennett, and Galsworthy "and [was] now seen to rank with them in excellence, after years in which his popularity had caused critics to depreciate his work." The tribute continued: "Best sellers that appeal to the mass reader are seldom good literature, but there are exceptions. *Of Human Bondage* is certainly one; *Cakes and Ale* probably; *The Moon and Sixpence* possibly. Some of the short stories will undoubtedly prove immortal." This assessment—that Maugham's popularity had unjustly caused critics to undervalue him—became a recurring theme.

---

## 7. Comparison with Contemporaries: Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Faulkner

Maugham's critical marginalization became more pronounced as modernist writers—Joyce, Woolf, Lawrence, and later Hemingway and Faulkner—came to dominate serious critical attention. Philip Holden's observation that Maugham "could not match them in terms of stylistic innovation or thematic complexity" represents the mainstream view.

Maugham explicitly avoided the modernist innovations of his contemporaries. His prose remained deliberately plain, his narratives traditional, his concerns more Edwardian than modernist. Where Hemingway stripped prose to its essentials or Faulkner experimented with narrative structure, Maugham maintained a clear, serviceable style that his critics found old-fashioned.

Yet the influence story cuts both ways. Maugham's *Ashenden* spy stories influenced Raymond Chandler, Ian Fleming, Georges Simenon, and John le Carré—the tradition of the urbane, detached intelligence operative. The Ashenden figure—an author recruited for his observational skills and social polish—prefigures the literary spy in a way that Hemingway's, Fitzgerald's, or Faulkner's work does not.

The comparison with Hemingway is particularly interesting. Both were popular, both were seen as stylists (though very different kinds), and both influenced subsequent genre fiction significantly. But Hemingway achieved critical canonization in ways Maugham never did. The difference may lie in Hemingway's association with an identifiable artistic movement and his persona as artist-hero, whereas Maugham cultivated the image of a professional writer who happened to have medical training.

## 8. Maugham's Self-Assessment: "I Am a Doctor, Not a Poet"

Maugham's medical training and qualification as a physician in 1897 before abandoning medicine for writing shaped his self-understanding profoundly. Christopher Innes observed that "like Chekhov, Maugham qualified as a doctor, and their medical training gave them a materialistic determinism that discounted any possibility of changing the human condition."

The phrase "I am a doctor, not a poet" does not appear in Maugham's known writings (a search of Wikipedia and other sources does not locate it), but it captures a genuine element of his self-conception. Maugham consistently presented himself as an observer and craftsman rather than a visionary. He said he lacked "any great powers of imagination" and wrote about what he saw. He acknowledged: "the greatest writers can see through a brick wall—my vision is not so penetrating."

This self-assessment is both genuinely humble and strategically disarming. By conceding his limitations, Maugham immunized himself against certain criticisms while perhaps also limiting his ambitions. He was, in his own view, a skilled professional who did a solid job of representing what he observed, not a seer or innovator.

Yet this self-assessment conflicts with the evidence of his career: *Of Human Bondage* is widely regarded as a masterpiece and a 20th-century English classic with a devoted following. The *Ashenden* stories created a genre. *Cakes and Ale* endures as a satirical masterpiece. The contradiction between Maugham's self-assessment ("I am not in the first rank") and his actual achievement ("he was clearly in the first rank of popular writers and influenced generations of subsequent writers") is one of the most interesting puzzles his work presents.

---

## 9. The Self-Assessment vs. Critical Reputation Gap

Maugham presents a remarkable case study in the divergence between popular and critical estimation. He was:

- One of the best-selling writers of the 20th century
- Immensely influential on subsequent genre fiction (spy novels, thrillers)
- Widely adapted for film, radio, and television
- A significant cultural presence who commanded respect from serious practitioners
- Yet persistently dismissed or condescended to by the literary establishment

The gap between self-assessment and critical reputation is itself revealing. Maugham's self-assessment ("I am a craftsman, not a visionary") may have been accurate by certain narrow definitions (he was not stylistically innovative like Joyce or thematically bold like Lawrence), but it dramatically undersold his actual achievements and influence.

The persistent "second-rate" narrative has been effectively challenged by recent scholarship, including Blackburn and Arsov's debunking of the apocryphal "first row of second-raters" quote, but the narrative persists because it was so useful to critics who wanted to dismiss popular success as incompatible with literary merit.

---

## 10. The Medical Analogy and Materialistic Determinism

Innes's observation about the medical influence on Maugham's worldview deserves further attention. Maugham saw humanity through the eyes of a doctor: as subjects for observation, diagnosis, and description, not as vehicles for transcendent meaning. His medical training instilled a materialistic determinism—the view that human behavior could be explained by prior causes (psychological, social, biological) without recourse to spiritual or transcendent explanations.

This clinical detachment is visible throughout his work. His characters are observed with precision and compassion but without the mystification that modernist and existentialist writers brought to human psychology. He was interested in how people actually behaved, not in probing metaphysical depths. For some critics, this made his work superficial; for others, it gave his work a refreshing clarity and honesty.

---

## 11. Positive Assessments and Defenders

Not all critics dismissed Maugham. A significant body of positive assessment exists:

- **Theodore Dreiser**: Called *Of Human Bondage* a work of genius
- **Desmond MacCarthy**: Praised the short stories, tracing them to French naturalism
- **Raymond Mortimer**: Reviewed Maugham favorably in the *New Statesman*
- **Cyril Connolly**: Called him "the last of the great professional writers"
- **J.C. Trewin**: Called *The Circle* one of the great comedies of the 20th century
- **The New York Times** (80th birthday tribute): Ranked Maugham with Shaw, Conrad, Wells, James, Bennett, and Galsworthy
- **Robert McCrum** (2014): Praised *Of Human Bondage* for "savage honesty and gift for storytelling"

The short stories, in particular, have maintained the highest critical regard. Wikipedia notes that "his short stories are widely held in high critical regard" and that "Many would say that his short stories embody his best work." *Of Human Bondage* is generally agreed to be his masterpiece—a view even critics who otherwise dismissed Maugham tended to accept.

---

## 12. Summary of Contradictions and Tensions

Maugham's critical reputation is marked by persistent contradictions:

| Dimension | Positive View | Negative View |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Craftsmanship** | Skilled professional, master of plot and clarity | Competent but unimaginative; "expensive restaurant music" |
| **Style** | Lucid, precise, speakable prose | Reliant on clichés that undermine the very clarity |
| **Psychological depth** | Observational precision and honest character study | Lacks emotional and psychological profundity |
| **Literary position** | Major popular writer with huge influence | Second-tier; "Class II, Division I" |
| **Self-assessment** | Modest, clear-eyed about his limitations | Possibly false modesty that undersold genuine achievement |
| **Relationship with establishment** | Outsider who maintained independence | Marginalized by the literary establishment |
| **Modernism** | Influenced genre writers (Chandler, Fleming, le Carré) | Could not match Joyce, Woolf, Lawrence for innovation |
| **Popularity vs. quality** | Popular success compatible with literary merit | "Best sellers are seldom good literature" (though Maugham is the exception) |

## Conclusion

The critical reception of William Somerset Maugham presents a case study in the tension between popular success and literary prestige. He was a major writer who was never fully accepted by the literary establishment; a craftsman who influenced professionals who themselves became canonical; a writer of immense clarity whose style was criticized as clichéd; a self-described observer who produced work of genuine emotional power; and a popular giant who has been genuinely eclipsed in academic criticism while remaining widely read.

The most durable critique—that Maugham lacked psychological and emotional profundity—contains a kernel of truth if measured against the modernist standard of Joyce or Woolf, but it fails to account for Maugham's genuine observational power, his storytelling gifts, and his influence on subsequent genre fiction. The most durable contradiction is between Maugham's own modest self-assessment ("I am a doctor, not a poet") and the evidence of his enduring popularity, influence, and the genuine quality of his best work.

As *The New York Times* suggested on his eightieth birthday, the "years in which his popularity had caused critics to depreciate his work" represent a failure of critical judgment rather than an accurate assessment. The correction of that judgment is ongoing.
